Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP)

India's First Line of Defense Against Epidemics

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • Entire population of India, particularly vulnerable groups in rural and urban slums.
  • Healthcare providers, public health authorities, surveillance officers, and labs.

Eligibility Criteria

  • No specific eligibility — surveillance covers all districts and disease-prone areas.
  • Healthcare providers and institutions are responsible for reporting notifiable diseases.

Introduction to the Scheme

The Integrated Disease Surveillance Programme (IDSP) was launched in 2004 to strengthen disease surveillance in India. It aims to detect and respond to outbreaks rapidly through IT-enabled systems and trained healthcare personnel. It plays a vital role in pandemic preparedness and monitoring public health threats.

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

Core Features:

  • Weekly surveillance data collection and outbreak alerts.
  • Three-tier reporting system (S, P, L forms) for comprehensive tracking.
  • Rapid Response Teams (RRTs) for immediate investigation and control.
  • Media scanning and verification cell (MSVC) for early warning from news sources.
  • Strengthened labs and trained staff for diagnosis and management.

Programme Components

Component Description
Surveillance Units Established at Central, State, and District levels for disease reporting and management.
Human Resource Development Training programs for Surveillance Officers, RRTs, healthcare staff.
ICT Usage Real-time data collection and collation via IT platforms.
Lab Strengthening Upgraded district/state labs for effective diagnostics.
Inter-sectoral Coordination Integration with animal health departments for zoonotic diseases.

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare (MoHFW), Government of India
  • Implemented by: National Centre for Disease Control (NCDC)
  • Support: World Bank and other technical partners (initial phase)

Application Process

  • Not applicable for citizens — healthcare facilities automatically report data to IDSP.
  • Public health officials and institutions follow reporting protocols weekly.

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched in November 2004
  • Ongoing under the National Health Mission
  • Expanded to all states and UTs with continuous upgrades

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Timely outbreak detection and control
  • Improved national health monitoring and infrastructure
  • Supports India’s obligations under International Health Regulations (IHR)

Weaknesses:

  • Reporting inconsistencies across states
  • Under-reporting and lack of trained manpower in remote areas
  • Delays in lab confirmations in some districts

Real-World Impact

  • Outbreak Tracking: Over 1,700 outbreaks reported annually in recent years.
  • Common Diseases Detected: Measles, Chickenpox, Food Poisoning, Chikungunya, ADD.
  • Media Alerts: Over 4,800 media alerts tracked (till Feb 2018) — diarrhea, dengue, measles being frequent alerts.

Through IDSP, India has greatly improved epidemic preparedness and real-time disease tracking, forming a critical part of its public health defense system.

BONUS: Strategic Insight

IDSP showcases how integrating tech, inter-sectoral coordination, and data-driven policymaking can transform epidemic surveillance. It's a robust model for pandemic preparedness and public health resilience in a country as vast and diverse as India.
