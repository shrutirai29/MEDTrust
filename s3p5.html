National AIDS Control Programme (NACP)

Combating HIV/AIDS Through Awareness, Treatment & Prevention

Target Group

  • High-risk groups: Female sex workers (FSWs), Men who have sex with men (MSM), Injecting drug users (IDUs), Migrants, Truckers
  • People living with HIV/AIDS (PLHIV)
  • Pregnant women and newborns (for prevention of transmission)
  • General population (for awareness & education)

Eligibility Criteria

  • No income, age, gender or caste restriction
  • All services are provided freely through public health systems
  • Focus is given to high-risk and vulnerable populations

Introduction to the Programme

The National AIDS Control Programme (NACP) was launched in 1992 to tackle the growing HIV/AIDS epidemic in India. It is implemented by the National AIDS Control Organization (NACO) and has evolved over multiple phases focusing on prevention, treatment, care, and strengthening of health systems.

What It Contains (Key Interventions & Benefits)

  • Targeted Interventions for high-risk groups
  • Free Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) at government centres
  • Prevention of Parent-To-Child Transmission services
  • Free HIV testing & counseling via ICTCs
  • Safe blood supply under National Blood Policy
  • HIV education for adolescents (NAEP)
  • Community involvement via GIPA strategy
  • Mainstreaming HIV response across ministries & civil society

Funding & Implementing Agencies

  • Funded by: Government of India, Global Fund, World Bank & other international partners
  • Implemented by: NACO, State AIDS Control Societies (SACS), District Units (DAPCUs)
  • Support Structures: TSUs, STRCs, SIMS (for technical help, training, and monitoring)

Application Process

  • Direct walk-in access at ART Centres, ICTCs, and testing facilities
  • No formal application or documentation needed for most services
  • Outreach programs actively connect high-risk individuals to care

Timeline & Phases

Phase Year Key Focus
NACP-I 1992–1999 Awareness, surveillance, safe blood, targeted interventions
NACP-II 1999–2006 Prevention-focused, ART rollout, blood policy, system building
NACP-III 2007–2012 Goal: Halting and reversing the epidemic
NACP-IV Post-2012 Sustainability, community ownership, convergence with health systems

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Free access to testing and treatment for all
  • Community-based, decentralized approach
  • Global recognition for model implementation

Challenges:

  • Stigma and discrimination persist in some regions
  • Logistics and ART stock-out issues in remote areas
  • Need for sustained funding and outreach

Real-World Impact

India has successfully reduced new HIV infections by over 50% since the peak in the early 2000s. The number of people receiving ART has steadily grown, improving life expectancy and quality of life. Despite achievements, gaps remain in reaching marginalized groups and improving ART adherence.

Vision Forward

The NACP aligns with the Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) target of ending AIDS as a public health threat by 2030. The programme continues to focus on prevention, education, and treatment access with greater community participation.
