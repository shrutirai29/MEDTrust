National Rabies Control Programme (NRCP)

Combating Rabies Through the One Health Approach

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • All individuals exposed to dog or other animal bites
  • Healthcare providers managing animal bite cases
  • Veterinarians, animal handlers, and municipal workers
  • Urban and rural communities at risk of rabies

Eligibility Criteria

  • No eligibility restrictions — all bite victims can access care
  • Focus on rabies-endemic regions of India
  • Special emphasis on low-income, high-risk populations

Introduction to the Scheme

Rabies is a highly fatal but vaccine-preventable disease. India accounts for nearly 36% of global rabies deaths, with an estimated 20,000 human deaths annually. Most cases result from dog bites (96%). The disease is endemic in almost all parts of India except Andaman & Nicobar Islands and Lakshadweep.

To address this, the National Rabies Control Programme (NRCP) was launched in 2013 during the 12th Five-Year Plan under the National Health Mission. It follows the One Health approach, recognizing the interconnection between human and animal health systems.

What It Contains (Components of the Programme)

Human Health Component

  • Implemented through the National Centre for Disease Control (NCDC)
  • Operational in 26 States and UTs
  • Budget allocation of ₹20 crores during the 12th Plan
  • Focus on post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) and training medical personnel

Animal Health Component

  • Managed by the Animal Welfare Board of India under the Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change
  • Pilot projects in Haryana and Chennai
  • Included Animal Birth Control (ABC) and dog vaccination strategies
  • Budget allocation of ₹30 crores. Concluded with the 12th Five-Year Plan in March 2017

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare and Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change
  • Implemented by: NCDC (human health) and AWBI (animal health)
  • Collaborative partners: State governments, municipal bodies, veterinary institutions

Application Process

  • No formal application required for bite victims
  • Treatment and vaccines are available at public health facilities
  • Protocols for bite case management are followed by health authorities

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched in 2013 under the 12th Five-Year Plan
  • Animal component concluded in 2017
  • Human component ongoing under NHM and NCDC coordination

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Integrated One Health model: links human and animal sectors
  • Focus on awareness, training, and vaccine access
  • Boosts rabies surveillance and diagnostics in India

Weaknesses:

  • Discontinuation of animal component after 12th Plan
  • Uneven implementation across states
  • Limited availability of rabies immunoglobulin in rural areas

Real-World Impact

  • Decentralized rabies prevention and case management
  • Wider access to vaccines and improved diagnostics
  • Widespread training of healthcare workers across states
  • Continued public education and behavioral change promotion

Way Forward

India is committed to the Global Strategic Plan to End Human Deaths from Dog-Mediated Rabies by 2030. NRCP aims to accelerate rabies elimination through:

  • Mass dog vaccination and Animal Birth Control (ABC) initiatives
  • Enhanced rabies surveillance systems
  • Public-private partnerships and increased funding
  • Expanded diagnostic infrastructure and community-level awareness
