National Leprosy Eradication Programme (NLEP)

Eradicating Leprosy, Empowering Lives

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • Individuals diagnosed with leprosy across India
  • Communities in endemic and high-risk regions
  • People with visible deformities or disabilities due to leprosy
  • Vulnerable groups in urban slums and remote rural areas

Eligibility Criteria

  • Anyone exhibiting symptoms of leprosy can access free services
  • No restrictions based on age, gender, caste, income, or residence
  • Walk-in diagnosis and care available at PHCs, CHCs, and district hospitals

Introduction to the Scheme

The National Leprosy Eradication Programme (NLEP) is a centrally sponsored scheme under the National Health Mission. Originally launched as the National Leprosy Control Programme in 1955, it was rebranded as NLEP in 1983 to emphasize eradication. The program focuses on interrupting transmission, ensuring early detection, and providing free treatment and rehabilitation services. The vision includes eliminating stigma and achieving “Zero Leprosy.”

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

  • Free diagnosis and complete treatment using WHO-recommended Multi-Drug Therapy (MDT)
  • Financial support for Reconstructive Surgeries (₹12,000 per patient)
  • Disability Prevention and Medical Rehabilitation (DPMR) services
  • Community outreach and awareness campaigns
  • Contact tracing and early case detection through ASHA workers
  • Capacity building of health staff and volunteers
  • Monitoring via indicators like New Case Detection Rate & Grade-II disability ratio

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health and Family Welfare, Government of India (100% Central Assistance)
  • Implemented by: Central Leprosy Division in collaboration with State Leprosy Cells
  • Support Partners: WHO, ILEP, and national NGOs

Application Process

  • No formal application required
  • Patients can directly visit government facilities for diagnosis and treatment
  • Active surveillance through health workers, especially ASHAs, and routine screening in endemic areas

Duration & Timeline

  • 1955: Launched as National Leprosy Control Programme
  • 1983: Renamed as National Leprosy Eradication Programme (NLEP)
  • 2005: India declared leprosy eliminated as a public health problem (less than 1 case per 10,000 population)
  • 2023–2027: Strategic plan launched to achieve Zero Leprosy by 2027

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Universal free access to high-quality diagnosis and care
  • Strong community-based interventions through ASHAs and health workers
  • Integration with primary healthcare system and international support

Challenges:

  • Persistent stigma and fear hinder self-reporting
  • Underreporting and missed cases in remote or marginalized communities
  • Access gaps for reconstructive surgery and disability services in some areas

Real-World Impact

Although India achieved leprosy elimination status in 2005, over 100,000 new cases are still detected annually, highlighting the need for continued efforts. The program has significantly reduced visible deformities through early detection. ASHA workers have been instrumental in promoting awareness and supporting case detection at the grassroots level. Monitoring indicators now guide targeted interventions, making the program increasingly data-driven.

BONUS: Strategic Vision 2023–2027

The Ministry of Health has laid out an ambitious roadmap to eliminate leprosy transmission entirely by 2027. This includes:

  • Zero transmission and disability through early detection
  • Integrated case management with improved DPMR services
  • Community sensitization and anti-stigma campaigns
  • Leveraging technology for real-time data collection and response
