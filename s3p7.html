National Viral Hepatitis Control Programme (NVHCP)

Towards a Hepatitis-Free India by 2030

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • People at high risk for Hepatitis B and C (e.g., people who inject drugs, those with HIV, dialysis patients, etc.)
  • Pregnant women in low-institutional delivery areas
  • General population at risk for Hepatitis A and E (e.g., areas with poor sanitation)
  • Healthcare workers and laboratory staff (especially for Hepatitis B vaccination)

Eligibility Criteria

  • No income, age, or residence restriction
  • Free screening and treatment available to all citizens via government healthcare facilities
  • Focus on early diagnosis and treatment adherence through digital tracking

Introduction to the Scheme

Launched on July 28, 2018 (World Hepatitis Day) by the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare, the NVHCP is a comprehensive public health response to eliminate viral hepatitis as a major public health threat by 2030. It addresses hepatitis A, B, C, D, and E through integrated strategies under the National Health Mission and supports India's commitment to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) 3.3.

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

Preventive Measures

  • Hepatitis B vaccination for newborns, healthcare workers, and high-risk groups
  • Awareness campaigns and community engagement
  • Blood safety, safe injections, and hygiene promotion

Diagnostic and Treatment Services

  • Free screening and confirmatory tests for Hepatitis B and C
  • Free antiviral treatment through public healthcare facilities
  • Screening of pregnant women in targeted areas

Digital and Support Services

  • Digital case-based tracking system
  • Community peer support models to improve adherence
  • Public-private partnerships for service delivery

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare, Government of India
  • Implemented by: State & District Health Departments under NHM
  • Collaborators: NCDC, ILBS, Department of Health Research, WHO

Application Process

  • No formal application needed by individuals
  • Eligible citizens receive services automatically at designated government health facilities
  • Digital tracking helps manage continuity of care and monitor outcomes

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched: July 28, 2018
  • Ongoing programme under the umbrella of the National Health Mission
  • Target Year: Elimination of Hepatitis C by 2030

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • First national program covering all 5 major hepatitis viruses
  • Integration with other national health services
  • Focus on both prevention and treatment
  • Digital surveillance and monitoring

Weaknesses:

  • Limited awareness in rural and remote populations
  • Challenges in identifying asymptomatic carriers
  • Gaps in treatment adherence and follow-up tracking
