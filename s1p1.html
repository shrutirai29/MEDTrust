Janani Shishu Suraksha Karyakaram (JSSK)

Empowering Mothers & Newborns Through Free Public Healthcare

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • Pregnant women delivering in public health institutions
  • Sick newborns (up to 30 days old)
  • Sick infants (0–1 year old)

Eligibility Criteria

  • Pregnant women delivering in government health facilities
  • Neonates (newborns) and infants up to 1 year requiring treatment in public institutions
  • No income, caste, or residence restriction

Introduction to the Scheme

The JSSK was launched in 2011 under the National Rural Health Mission (NRHM) to eliminate out-of-pocket expenses for pregnant women and newborns accessing public healthcare. It follows the Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY), with the goal of improving institutional deliveries and reducing maternal and infant mortality rates.

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

For Pregnant Women:

  • Free normal and C-section deliveries
  • Free drugs and consumables
  • Free diagnostics (blood tests, ultrasounds, etc.)
  • Free diet during stay (3 days for normal delivery, 7 days for C-section)
  • Free blood provision
  • Free transport (to hospital, referral, return home)

For Sick Newborns & Infants:

  • Free treatment in public hospitals (up to 1 year)
  • Free drugs, diagnostics, transport, blood, and diet

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare, Government of India
  • Implemented by: State Health Missions & District Health Societies
  • Fund Flow: Via NRHM flexi-pool

Application Process

  • No formal application is needed
  • Services are automatically provided at government health facilities
  • Protocols and logistics are managed by health authorities

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched in 2011
  • Ongoing scheme under NHM
  • Expanded in 2013 to cover infants up to 1 year

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Reduces out-of-pocket health expenses
  • Encourages institutional deliveries
  • Promotes equity for rural, tribal, and poor women

Weaknesses:

  • Implementation varies by state
  • Drug, transport, and blood shortages in some areas
  • Lack of awareness in remote regions

Real-World Impact

Since JSSK was launched, institutional deliveries have significantly increased and maternal and infant mortality rates have decreased in many parts of India. However, regional inequalities still exist and need continuous attention.

BONUS: Strategic Insight

JSSK combines demand-side incentives with supply-side service assurance — a powerful public health strategy. If implemented effectively, it can become the backbone of maternal and child health equity in India.
