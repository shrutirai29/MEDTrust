Pulse Polio Immunization Programme

India’s Landmark Mission to Eradicate Poliomyelitis

Target Group

  • All children below 5 years of age, irrespective of socio-economic status

Eligibility Criteria

  • All children under the age of 5 years residing in India
  • No restrictions based on caste, income, or region

Introduction to the Scheme

Launched in 1995, the Pulse Polio Programme was a national immunization campaign to eradicate poliomyelitis in India. The strategy involved mass administration of oral polio vaccine (OPV) to all children under 5 years to interrupt transmission of wild poliovirus. India was officially declared polio-free by the WHO in 2014 after years of persistent effort and multi-stakeholder collaboration.

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

Vaccines Used:

  • Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV): A live attenuated vaccine offering community-wide protection through herd immunity.
  • Inactivated Polio Vaccine (IPV): Introduced in 2015 for enhanced protection through injectable immunization.

Services Provided:

  • Free administration of OPV during National Immunization Days (NIDs) and Sub-National Immunization Days (SNIDs)
  • House-to-house vaccination to ensure full coverage
  • Booth-based mass immunization on designated days
  • Rapid response mop-up operations in areas of detected polio cases

Funding & Implementing Agencies

  • Funded by: Government of India in collaboration with global partners
  • Key Partners: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare (MoHFW), WHO, UNICEF, Rotary International, CDC
  • Technical Support: National Polio Surveillance Project (NPSP)

Application Process

  • No formal application is needed
  • Children under 5 are automatically eligible and covered during campaigns
  • Vaccination booths are set up in schools, hospitals, anganwadi centers, and public spaces

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched: 1995
  • Last recorded polio case: 2011 (West Bengal)
  • India declared polio-free: 2014
  • Ongoing as a precautionary strategy under Universal Immunization Programme

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Comprehensive national coverage through community-level engagement
  • Strong political will and inter-agency coordination
  • Public awareness campaigns like "Do Boond Zindagi Ki"

Challenges:

  • Initial vaccine hesitancy and misinformation
  • Difficulty accessing remote and mobile populations
  • Maintaining immunity and surveillance post-eradication

Real-World Impact

The Pulse Polio Programme is hailed as one of India’s most successful public health campaigns. It demonstrated the power of mass mobilization, community participation, and sustained public-private partnership. With India now polio-free, the country serves as a training hub and vaccine supplier to nations still fighting the disease.

BONUS: Strategic Insight

This initiative showcases how targeted immunization campaigns can overcome even the most persistent viral diseases. The Pulse Polio Programme set a precedent for pandemic preparedness, vaccine rollout, and public health communication in India.
