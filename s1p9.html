National Programme for Family Planning

India's pioneering reproductive health initiative

Target Group (To Whom It Applies)

  • Men and women of reproductive age, especially married couples.
  • Focus on:
    • Young couples
    • Postpartum/post-abortion women
    • High-fertility districts
    • Adolescents (via RMNCH+A)
  • ASHAs and community workers help with outreach and service delivery.

Eligibility Criteria

  • Open to all individuals/couples of reproductive age.
  • Voluntary participation — no restrictions based on income, caste, or region.
  • Focus on areas with high fertility and poor contraceptive access.

Introduction to the Scheme

The National Programme for Family Planning was launched in 1952 as the world’s first national-level family planning program. It evolved from a population control initiative into a comprehensive reproductive health program aimed at improving health outcomes through family planning and contraceptive services. This scheme is part of the RMNCH+A framework under the National Health Mission.

What It Contains (Benefits Offered)

Spacing Methods:

  • Condoms, OCPs, injectables (Antara), IUCDs

Permanent Methods:

  • Male (NSV) & Female sterilization

Post-pregnancy options:

  • PPIUCD, PAIUCD, PPS

Additional Benefits:

  • Free services at government health facilities, including counseling and distribution of contraceptives.
  • Campaigns like World Population Day and Vasectomy Fortnight for awareness.
  • FP-LMIS ensures proper contraceptive logistics.

Funding & Implementing Agency

  • Funded by: Ministry of Health & Family Welfare (MoHFW)
  • Implemented by: National Health Mission, State/District Health Departments, NGOs, and private providers

Application Process

  • No formal application required.
  • Free services at government health facilities including Sub-Centres, PHCs, CHCs, and District Hospitals.
  • ASHAs/ANMs provide counseling and distribute contraceptives.

Duration & Timeline

  • Launched in 1952 — still active.
  • Mission Parivar Vikas launched in 2017 to accelerate efforts and introduce new options like Centchroman pills and injectables.

Pros & Limitations

Strengths:

  • Voluntary, rights-based program
  • Diverse contraceptive options
  • ASHAs bridge rural gaps
  • Improved male engagement in some areas

Weaknesses:

  • Low male participation
  • Service gaps in remote regions
  • Occasional stock-outs of contraceptives
  • Cultural resistance, myths still prevalent

Real-World Impact

Since its inception, India’s fertility rate has steadily declined, with a significant increase in modern contraceptive usage among married women. The program, especially through Mission Parivar Vikas, has improved access to family planning services in high-focus states. However, an unmet need persists in adolescents and underserved groups.
